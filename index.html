What we miss is thinking of it as a design problem

I think a lot of you come from design and other creative disciplines. Design thinking applied

to data-visualization is…

When you apply design thinking to data, you get data storytelling. It’s a buzzword now, like “insights,” you can just throw it around. So I think it’s worth being specific about what kind of storytelling we’re actually talking about.

Data Storytelling

There are two ways to think about a story. And the distinction matters a lot for how intimidating this feels.

STORY

A sequence of events. A protagonist. Conflict, resolution.

Novels, films, plays, essays.

The first is the capital-S Story. This is what most frameworks are talking about. A sequence of events, a protagonist, conflict, resolution. Novels, films, essays. A traditional narrative arc.

And you’ve probably heard about The Pudding, who do this brilliantly. When I was in college and I looked at their work, I felt completely overwhelmed. How do I get from my small charts to this? It used to scare me.

story

Something clear to say, in a form that reaches someone.

Whatever that form may be.

But there’s also the lowercase story. And I think this is where most of us are actually working. I define it as having something specific and clear to say, in a form that can reach someone. The output doesn’t need a grand arc. It just needs a solid point of view.

FT: A new global gender divide is emerging

This chart by John Burn-Murdoch is a good example. It’s about how political ideologies are diverging between men and women across the world. But this chart which is a fairly simple set of graphics with just a strong headline feels complete to me. I know what he wants me to see. I know something I didn’t know before. That’s a story.

Not Ship: Last call for third places?

Someone writing about how third spaces were actually rising before the pandemic, then declined after. Very simple charts. What does the heavy lifting is the annotations and the headline. It contains everything I need to know about what this person wanted me to understand.

And then there’s this. Spotify Wrapped isn’t even charts. But everybody looks forward to it. It’s a data experience, presented in an incredibly intentional way. This is data storytelling too. And I think it’s worth taking seriously as an example.

When you think more of the

second kind of story, a lot of

possibilities open up.

When you start thinking in lowercase stories, two things happen. First, a lot more possibilities open up because the form is your choice. Second, it actually feels attainable. I can make something like Spotify Wrapped. I might not be able to make The Pudding just yet, but I can curate something intentional and specific.

Big Stories can come together when one has clarity on the smaller stories


And once you nail your small stories, your small charts with a clear point of view, you can start assembling them into something larger. The lowercase story is not a consolation prize.

Our ‘stories’ can even live inside the

small, understated dataviz we make

This applies to the small everyday charts we make — for assignments, for clients, for fun. The story can live inside something very simple. But only if you’ve committed to what it’s about.

Butttt, not every viz is a story.

The data storytelling process has two distinct phases:

exploration and explanation.


Sometimes we mistake showing readers our exploration

when they came for an explanation.

But, and this is important, not every visualization is a story. The data storytelling process has two distinct phases: exploration and explanation. Exploration is what you do for yourself. Explanation is what the reader is expecting when they engage with your work. The mistake I made constantly when I was learning was showing readers my exploration when they came for an explanation. I was proud of the work. I wanted them to see all of it. But what they saw was a pile of rocks, not a story.

Here’s an early example of mine. This is from Common Sense Media — they review movies for parents and one of the things they do is tag characters as positive or negative role models. Red is male, blue is female. There are a lot of design problems here.

But looking back, the bigger problem is that I never committed to saying what I noticed. There’s a clear pattern, but I asked the reader to find it themselves instead of just telling them.

This is more recent where I was analyzing how repetitive each Taylor Swift album is. Better design, better color, annotations. But the same problem. If you’re not a Taylor Swift fan, your first question is: so what? And the thing is I can answer it! But for some reason I didn’t

Okay, and?

What do I want you to take away?

“Okay, and?” is the most useful question you can ask of your own work. If you can’t answer it with a single clear sentence — what is the point of this, what do you want me to take away — you have an exploration, not an explanation. The synthesis is converting one into the other.

As Swift moved into mainstream pop to write catchy songs,

her lyrics naturally became more repetitive…

The answer here isn’t complicated. As Taylor Swift moved into more mainstream pop to write catchy songs, her lyrics naturally became more repetitive — that’s just the nature of those songs. That’s it. I just needed to say it. I just didn’t commit to it.

A story reduces exploration until it fits in a sentence.


What did you see?

How would you want to tell me about it?

A story becomes an explanation when you can fit it into one sentence. What did you see? How would you want to tell me about it? That’s the whole question.

Making a ‘story’

So let’s talk about actually making one. I’ll walk through the process I use, using the election project as the running example.

1. Call to Action

The first step is finding the thing you want to know badly enough to spend a week measuring. This is not opening a spreadsheet. This is finding the itch.

A call to action can come from anywhere.

  1. Curiosity about something you read
  2. An argument you want to win
  3. A shared joke
  4. Assignments (welp)

At its worst, it’s interesting only to you, which is still a win.

At its best, it’s a relatable hook that someone would spend time on.

It can come from something you read, an argument you want to win, a shared joke, or yes, an assignment. At its worst, it’s interesting only to you — which is still a win, because if you care about what you’re visualizing, you’ll have something to say about it. At its best, it’s a hook someone else would spend time on. It’s worth thinking about this phase intentionally.

Something looked odd in the data, which lead me to read about cases that were reported in the news

For the election project, it started with a conversation. I noticed names looking similar in some data I was poking at. My friend said — yeah, this is actually a known thing. I started reading about it in the news. And I couldn’t find anyone who’d measured how often it happens. That gap became the call to action.

Call to actions become questions


How many times has it happened?

How often does it confuse people?

What are noteworthy instances?

→ Counting occurrences

→ Average votes taken by namesakes

→ Extreme examples of small vote margins

A call to action then becomes a set of measurable questions. How many times has this happened? That’s countable. How often does it confuse people? That’s vague — so I translate it into something I can measure, like the average number of votes taken by namesake candidates. What are the noteworthy instances? That becomes: filter for the extreme cases where the margin was the smallest.

When we’re starting out, we tend to ask questions that are both vague and trivial. That’s fine because it’s the starting point. The goal is to move to questions that matter and are measurable. You can ask questions that matter but are vague, and that won’t do anything for you. So the direction is: make it specific enough to count first, then ask the deeper layer of why.

Simple questions — how many, what’s the distribution, what’s the comparison — open the door to deeper questions. Why does it happen? What’s the consequence? Because this happened, therefore this happened. You start with something trivial and countable, and it leads you somewhere interesting.

I am curious about [Topic],

so I will measure [Data]

to see if [Hypothesis] is true.

Adapted from Andrew Duckworth

A simple test: if you can finish this sentence, you have a good question. “I am curious about X, so I will measure Y to see if Z is true.” For mine: I’m curious about namesake candidates, so I will count the votes they receive to see if this actually affects an election result. If you can fill that in, you’re ready to start.

2. Analysis

Analysis used to scare me because I’m not trained in statistics. What I eventually learned is that most interesting findings come from four very simple operations.

As little as four operations.

  1. Count - How many?
  2. Filter - Which ones?
  3. Sort - What’s biggest or smallest?
  4. Group - How do categories compare?

How many? Which ones? What’s the biggest or smallest? How do categories compare? These four, in combination, will get you almost everything you need. Or at least what is bare minimum of interest to a reader.

And these can be composed, meaning you can combine them:

  1. First filter for something
  2. Group it into categories
  3. Count items within categories

And you can chain them. Filter for a subset, group it into categories, count within those categories. That’s a three-step analysis and it will produce genuinely interesting findings on almost any dataset.

Simple (but shocking) example

Someone catalogued 603 episodes of Scooby Doo, let’s count how many times Daphne catches a monster…



caught_by_daphne total_episodes
29 603

Simple example. Someone watched all 603 episodes of Scooby-Doo and catalogued variables — including who catches the monster in each episode. Daphne is famously the damsel in distress. So I just counted: how many episodes does she actually catch the monster? In almost 95% of episodes, Daphne is not the one catching anything. That single count, by itself, tells a story about how the show is written. We could then compare with Velma, compare male and female characters overall. But it started with just counting.

Largest, Smallest, Most, Least…


How Bangalore Uses The Metro

Filtering for extremes is also very useful. In this story about Bangalore’s metro, I was looking at average ridership on any given route. And I found that on one specific route — station A to station B to station D — there was only one person traveling that unique path every day. Versus 300 people on any average pair of stations. That’s a finding. And it came from just sorting to find the bottom of a list.

If nothing interesting appears,

that’s a signal to revisit the question,

not to run fancier statistics.

When analysis returns nothing interesting, that usually means the question wasn’t specific enough, or the proxy wasn’t right. Go back to the question.

3. Synthesis

You’ve asked a question, built a measurement, run your operations. Now you have a finding. Synthesis is two moves: declare what you found, and choose the form that reveals it.

Take the metro data. I could present it as: “distribution of daily subway ridership by station by hour for the entire city.” That’s accurate. That’s what I found. It interests approximately no one outside of a transport planning meeting.

Or I could ask: do you want to know if your work commute is like everyone else’s? Are you that one person on a unique route, or are you one of 300? Now it’s personal. Now the reader has a reason to care. The data is the same. The synthesis is different. That’s the whole job.

“If a story is not about the hearer he will not listen… The strange and foreign is not interesting — only the deeply personal and familiar”

— John Steinbeck, East of Eden

Steinbeck said, in the context of fiction, that if a story is not about the hearer, they will not listen. I think this applies completely to data. The reader is the point.

Readers probably want to know…

  • Normals - Describe what “typical” looks like
  • Comparison - How does one count compare to another?
  • Trends - Are things going up, going down, staying the same?
  • Exceptions - What’s surprising? Some outliers?

Your findings tend to take one of four shapes: normals, comparisons, trends, or exceptions. Knowing which one you have tells you a lot about the form it wants to take.

And we don’t even have to use a chart at all. In the election story, I found one election with the most extreme case of namesake candidates — a ballot where one candidate was facing eleven other people with nearly the same name. Chandu Lal Sahu facing ten or eleven other Chandu Lal Sahus. When I found this in the data, I was genuinely amused. I told my illustrator friend about it and said — I don’t want to show this with a chart. And he came back with ideas.

He asked: what if it was like a scratch card, where you shuffle and you don’t know which Chandu you’re going to get? Or like a candy machine where there are duplicates? I loved the scratch card idea because it captured exactly what it would feel like to not know who the real candidate is — especially if you’re an illiterate voter trying to recognize a name on a ballot.

So we built this interaction where the cards shuffle and we ask you to keep track of who you think the real one is. It’s not proving anything analytically. It’s trying to let someone feel, for a moment, how confusing this could actually be.

I had also calculated a number for how similar two names are.


Names Similarity Face Face
Aman / Raman 0.9
Aman / John 0.0

I had also calculated a similarity score for how alike two names are, as part of the analysis. I was trying to figure out how to turn that number into a visual. My partner — who’s an illustrator — suggested: what if names that score high generate faces that look similar, and names that score low generate faces that look different? Aman and Raman, very similar names, very similar faces. Aman and John, very different.

So that’s what we built. It’s one form of encoding — but it came from having a specific finding and asking what form would make it feel real to someone.

  • Animations
  • Illustration and hand-drawn visuals
  • Interactive experiences
  • Scrollytelling

The form is the last decision, and it is entirely yours.

Animation, illustration, interactive experiences, scrollytelling, sound — these are all available to you. The form is the last decision, and it is entirely yours. What matters is that you’ve asked a good question, done the analysis, and synthesized what you found. After that, the visual is genuinely up to you.

Pick the tool that removes the most

friction between you and the question.

The tool is not the point. The question is the point. Pick whatever gets you to an answer fastest even if it’s not the most powerful tool available. Upgrade when the question demands it, not before.
