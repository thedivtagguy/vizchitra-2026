I like the idea of a Story and a story proposed by Cole Nussbaumer Kanflic. The difference is subtle.

A Story with a capital S is a story in the form that we're used to in books and movies. It is an overarching narrative, a grand once upon a time with a protagonist and a set of characters that go from some conflict to resolution. A lot of excellent data journalism falls in this category.

This isn't always easy to do, and sometimes maybe not everything needs a Story.

# The Anatomy of a story

## The Question

The 'Aha!' for a reader usually doesn't come from the chart or the math.

It happens when we recognize a relatable human experience reflected inside the abstraction.

---

> “If a story is not about the hearer he [or she] will not listen . . . A great lasting story is about everyone or it will not last. The strange and foreign is not interesting--only the deeply personal and familiar.”  ― John Steinbeck, East of Eden

---

For that to happen, at its core, a story needs a good _question_.

the story needs to inform, and information is an answer. What it must be followed by is a good question.

## Measurement

> “Data! Data! Data! I can't make bricks without clay”

Once we have our question and catalyst, need to need to figure out how to quantify it.

---

Your question can be clearly quantifiable, if the data is clear.

---

...or sometimes you may need to think about how to measure it.

In such cases, we can ideate metrics that will help us arrive at our answer.

---

For example, what if I asked you which characters are the worst-off in Scooby Doo?

This is a pretty vague question because it depends on what we mean by 'worst-off'.

1. Has the least amount of lines.
2. Gets attacked the most.
3. Has the poorest diet control.

---

If we rephrase this question to be more specific: Are female characters in Scooby-Doo more likely to be saved, or be the ones saving?


This question, to me, is interesting because it asks something deeper about our subjects. It is specific enough to interest a Scooby-Doo enthusiast, but one layer down, is about gender and agency in the stories we tell children. Scooby-Doo just happens to be a useful, specific, countable place to look for an answer.

> "out of numerous particulars the universal becomes evident".

---

The next step would be to figure out if this has already been measured or do you need to measure it yourself. In our case, we have it measured.

I'll skip the analysis here but I counted how many times each character gets captured in each episode and the resulting chart paints a clear picture of who gets captured and who does the capturing of monsters.

### Proxies

I'll show you one more example of a project which started off with a question that had an unclear measurement.

I was working on making a dashboard for Elections in India when I saw that a lot of political candidates, who were standing for elections, often had two or three people on the same ballot who sounded very much like the official candidate of the bigger party.

To put this in your perspective, imagine you're voting. On the ballot you see:

Donald Trump
Donald Drump
Joe Biden

You'd probably catch it. But what if you'd only heard the name before, never seen it written? What if you were voting in a second language? What if there were eleven of them?

---

1. Is this being done deliberately?
2. Do similarly-named candidates confuse voters? _Okay but how?_
4. Have similarly-named candidates drawn enough votes to change the outcome of an election?

That last question goes one level deeper by thinking concretely about what I would measure to answer question #2.

## Analysis

Before I got into this scene, I was terrible at math. I was concerned that being in design hampered my opportunities when I wanted to join a team like Reuters, which did data analysis and visualization for the global scale. I don't know statistics, what next for me? Do I need to become an expert _first_?

While there is endless breadth you can cover in analysis, four things can take you 90% of the way in being able to tell an interesting story:

1. Counting _Measuring magnitude_
2. Filtering _Focusing on a subset_
3. Sorting _Finding extremes_
4. Grouping _Bucketing and finding patterns within_

---

These four data analysis tools and techniques are available in whatever software/tool you use but they are incredibly effective at finding interesting things to talk about from a dataset at hand.
